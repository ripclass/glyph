## 1. The woman this is built for

A 24-year-old in a village in Mymensingh, pregnant with her second child. Her
first ANC visit happened because a community health worker passed through; there
was no second visit. Her blood pressure has been climbing for three weeks and no
one knows, because no one has measured it. When the headache and the swelling
come at eight months, her family will do exactly what the national mortality
surveys say families do: they will leave home and seek help — first the village
doctor, then the union facility that cannot handle eclampsia, then the upazila
hospital that has no record of her and no magnesium sulfate ready, then,
finally, too late, the district hospital. She will have done everything right.
The system will have made her ricochet.

The single most important research finding in this document is that this story
is the documented norm: the women who die of preeclampsia and eclampsia in
Bangladesh almost all *sought care*. They died in transit between facilities
that did not know them. Glyph Maa is built to end the ricochet.

## 2. The evidence: a celebrated decline that has stalled, and where it stalled

**The trajectory, honestly stated.** Bangladesh's maternal mortality decline is
one of global health's real success stories: from 322 deaths per 100,000 live
births (1998-2001) to 194 (2007-2010) — a 40% fall in under a decade, driven by
family planning, facility access, education, and economic improvement. But the
published literature now uses one word for the period since: *plateaued*. The
2016 BMMS measured essentially no progress from 2010, and JOGH projections
(2024) show the SDG target of MMR below 70 arriving by 2026 only in the
best-case scenario — and not until 2049 on the reference forecast. With current
MMR estimates in the 123-196 range depending on source and method, and roughly
three million live births a year, Bangladesh loses on the order of 4,000-6,000
mothers annually. The decline's easy gains are spent; what remains is the hard
core, and the surveys tell us exactly what it is made of.

**The stalled cause is the detectable one.** Preeclampsia/eclampsia-specific
mortality fell sharply from 77 per 100,000 live births (2001 BMMS) to 40
(2010 BMMS) — then *stopped*, registering 46 in the 2016 BMMS and rising to 24%
of all maternal deaths, back to its 2001 share. Seventy-six percent of these
deaths occur in rural areas; the deaths cluster in the first trimester, during
delivery, and within 48 hours postpartum. Hemorrhage, the other leading killer,
declined 35% over 2001-2010 and remains a major cause. Here is what makes the
preeclampsia number an indictment rather than a fate: the condition announces
itself through blood pressure — a measurement a trained neighbor can take with
a ৳2,500 cuff — weeks before it kills. The cause of death that has stopped
declining is the one most detectable by routine monitoring. That is not a
treatment gap. It is a surveillance gap.

**The care-seeking finding that defines the product.** The BMMS 2016 verbal
autopsies, analyzed in JOGH (2023), found that almost all women who died of
preeclampsia/eclampsia had left home to seek care — and most visited more than
one facility before dying. The authors' phrase is "an unprepared health
system." Each facility in the chain met the dying woman as a stranger: no
history, no BP trend, no warning she was coming, no certainty it could treat
her when she arrived. The deaths the decline cannot reach are routing and
information deaths.

**The coverage gaps are gradients, and they are steep.** UNICEF's disparity
profile quantifies the rural penalty: four-or-more ANC visits — 43.2% urban
versus 17.9% rural; skilled attendant at birth — 60.5% versus 35.6%;
institutional delivery — 56.8% versus 30.6%; postnatal care within two days —
74.7% versus 52.3%. Earlier BDHS analysis found just 35.9% of deliveries
nationally attended by skilled personnel (2014), with home delivery near 74% in
rural study populations, and wealth quintile the strongest single predictor.
One in three Bangladeshi women aged 20-24 gave birth before 18 — adolescent
pregnancies carrying the highest preeclampsia risk into the thinnest coverage.
The system Bangladesh built reaches the urban, the educated, the wealthier; the
deaths concentrate where it doesn't.

**The delivery workforce that exists.** What rural Bangladesh has is not
obstetricians but a lattice of community health workers — government FWAs and
HAs, NGO CHWs in icddr,b/BRAC tradition — and a legacy of traditional birth
attendants still conducting a substantial share of deliveries. The maternal
literature consistently identifies CHW contact as the strongest available
instrument for ANC uptake. Any maternal product that requires new clinical
workforce is fantasy; the design constraint is to make the existing lattice
clinically sharper.

## 3. The product: what Maa does

Glyph Maa is pregnancy-long, schedule-driven, and built around one conviction
from the evidence: the mother must stop being a stranger to the system that
will one day receive her in crisis.

**Enrollment at first contact, wherever that is.** A Chamber doctor, a Maa-
equipped CHW, an Apa in a garment factory (the pregnant operator from document
06), or self-enrollment through Pocket — any touchpoint creates the pregnancy
record: LMP, EDD, parity, risk factors, and a wallet that now carries a care
schedule. The WHO-aligned ANC contact schedule becomes WhatsApp reminders in
plain Bangla to the mother *and her family circle* — because the husband and
mother-in-law decide whether she travels to a visit, and the Pocket family-
circle architecture finally gives the deciders the information.

**BP surveillance as the spine (aimed at the stalled cause).** Maa's central
clinical loop is blood-pressure capture at every contact — CHW visit, pharmacy
corner, factory medical room, a neighbor's cuff — written to the wallet as
structured, source-tagged readings. KhaM-Med watches the trend, not the single
value: a climbing trajectory triggers escalation per national preeclampsia
protocol — urgent facility referral, with the receiving facility (where
Glyph-connected) seeing her record and her warning *before she arrives*. The
2016 BMMS timing cluster (first trimester, delivery, 48 hours postpartum)
defines the surveillance calendar: Maa does not stop at delivery; the postnatal
48-hour window gets its own contact schedule, and the preconception gap the
JOGH authors flag is addressed for known hypertensive women planning pregnancy.

**The routing layer (ending the ricochet).** When escalation fires, Maa does
what no amount of awareness messaging can: it answers *where*. Which reachable
facility has comprehensive emergency obstetric care, which has her record,
which is being notified now. The structured handover that Continuity builds for
migrant workers is the same artifact here — compressed history, current
readings, medications, risk flags — delivered to the receiving clinician in the
Bangladeshi Rx-culture format. The woman who today arrives as a stranger at her
third facility arrives, instead, as an expected patient at the right first one.

**The CHW instrument.** Maa's field surface is the assisted channel from the
Pocket paper, specialized: a CHW with a phone and a cuff runs a guided visit —
BP, weight, fundal height prompts, danger-sign checklist in Bangla, voice
notes — with KhaM-Med structuring everything into the wallet. The CHW gains
what the HERhealth evidence (Apa paper) says peers need to be effective:
a clinical backbone behind a trusted face. TBAs are not pretended away;
where a TBA attends, Maa's danger-sign escalation is designed to be usable by
her too, because the 35.6% rural skilled-attendance figure means the unskilled
attendant is, for now, who is actually in the room.

**The newborn's first credential.** Delivery closes the pregnancy record and
opens a new one: the child's DID, birth weight, delivery details, immunization
schedule seeded — the birth-certificate anchor path from document 10, and the
EPI reminder stream that rides the same family-circle rails. A health identity
that begins at birth is the long arc of the whole Glyph thesis, and Maa is
where it starts.

## 4. Why this needs the identity layer

The BMMS finding is an identity finding. Women died visiting multiple
facilities *because no facility knew them* — the record did not travel, so the
warning could not either. A ministry database could in principle fix this for
facilities it covers; it cannot fix it across the real care landscape the
verbal autopsies describe — village doctors, private clinics, NGO posts, public
hospitals — which will never share one database but can all verify one signed
credential. The mother's wallet is the only record architecture that is
guaranteed to be wherever she is. And the privacy stakes are not abstract:
pregnancy status disclosed to a garment-factory employer is a firing risk
(Apa paper); an adolescent pregnancy disclosed to the wrong party is a safety
risk. Patient-held keys with family-circle consent — her choosing *which*
family — is the only design that serves both the mother who wants her husband
informed and the one who must not have him informed.

## 5. Economics and the funding pathway

Maa is free to mothers, permanently — the 73% OOP reality (Pocket paper) and
the wealth-gradient in skilled attendance make any paywall a death gradient.
Maa is the ecosystem's flagship grant object, and the funder map is the
densest in global health: UNICEF and UNFPA (whose own disparity data documents
the gap Maa targets), the Gates Foundation (maternal health and digital health
portfolios), WHO Bangladesh, and the Government of Bangladesh's own DGHS/
DGFP programs, for which BMMS-measured preeclampsia mortality is a standing
failure. The fundable pilot is sharply defined: one upazila, every identified
pregnancy enrolled, CHW-run BP surveillance, escalation routing to a prepared
receiving facility — measured against the district's BMMS-pattern baseline.
Realistic dedicated funding: $3-8M across the maternal-health funder set for a
demonstration at upazila-to-district scale.

**The impact claim, with its arithmetic shown.** Bangladesh's total annual
maternal deaths number roughly 4,000-6,000 (≈3M live births × MMR 123-196 per
100,000). Preeclampsia/eclampsia's 24% share is roughly 1,000-1,400 of them.
A surveillance-and-routing intervention that reached national scale and
performed exceptionally might prevent some meaningful fraction of those — which
is why this document's claim is *hundreds of lives per year at scale, low
thousands across all causes in the best case*, and never more. Earlier internal
drafts carried larger numbers; they were wrong, the arithmetic above is why,
and the correction stands. Hundreds of mothers is not a small number. It does
not need inflating.

## 6. Honest constraints

- **Maa detects and routes; it does not treat.** Eclampsia is stopped by
  magnesium sulfate and emergency obstetric care, not by software. If the
  receiving facility lacks readiness — the BMMS authors' exact concern — Maa
  delivers a well-documented patient into the same unprepared room. Pilot
  design must therefore include facility-readiness verification (which
  facilities actually stock MgSO4, actually staff CEmOC) in the routing table,
  and the honest acknowledgment that Maa's ceiling is set by facility quality
  it does not control.
- **The BP cuff must physically exist.** Surveillance presumes hardware at the
  contact points. The pilot budget buys cuffs; the scale model rides existing
  CHW programs' equipment or grant-funded distribution. No cuff, no spine.
- **The family circle is double-edged.** The husband who gets the reminder may
  enable the visit — or control the information. Consent defaults and shielded
  modes (the Pocket architecture) are not optional features here; for
  adolescent and at-risk mothers they are the product.
- **TBA-usable escalation is hard and unproven.** Designing danger-sign flows a
  non-literate attendant will actually trigger is a research problem the pilot
  must test, not assume.
- **Voice and dialect constraints carry over** from Continuity: the rural
  mother's voice note is sensitive-category data, restricted until on-device
  processing lands (document 11).

## 7. Build status (June 2026)

Maa is designed, not built. It inherits Pocket's wallet and family circles,
the assisted-capture flow (Continuity/Apa), the WhatsApp service (already
implemented in the current build), the matching layer, and the identity lift
(document 10). Unbuilt and Maa-specific: the pregnancy-schedule engine, the BP
trend-and-escalation logic against national protocol, the facility-readiness
routing table, the CHW guided-visit flow, and the newborn enrollment path.
Sequenced for Year 2, after Chamber and Pocket prove the record loop and the
first grant funding lands — with the explicit understanding that Maa is the
document the funders will read first, because it is where the mission is
plainest: the woman in Mymensingh, the climbing pressure no one measured, and
the three facilities that met her as a stranger.
